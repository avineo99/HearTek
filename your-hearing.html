Your Hearing

Understanding your hearing is the first step toward better hearing health. At HearTek, we are committed to raising awareness about hearing loss, hearing protection, and early intervention. Our goal is to empower you with expert knowledge on how the ear functions, the common symptoms of hearing impairment, and the best practices for maintaining lifelong hearing health.

Hearing loss affects millions of people worldwide, yet many individuals delay seeking help. By staying informed, you can take proactive steps to preserve your hearing ability, prevent noise-induced hearing damage, and improve your overall quality of life.

How Does Your Ear Work?

Your ear is a complex organ responsible for detecting sound, processing speech, and maintaining balance. It is a highly intricate system that works seamlessly to help you interact with the world around you. The ear is divided into three primary sections, each playing a vital role in hearing and equilibrium:

  • Outer Ear (Pinna & Ear Canal) – Captures sound waves from the environment and funnels them through the ear canal to the eardrum.
  • Middle Ear (Eardrum & Ossicles) – The eardrum vibrates when hit by sound waves, transferring these vibrations to three tiny bones (ossicles: malleus, incus, and stapes), which amplify the sound.
  • Inner Ear (Cochlea & Auditory Nerve) – The cochlea, a fluid-filled structure lined with hair cells, translates sound vibrations into electrical signals. These signals travel through the auditory nerve to the brain, where they are processed as recognizable sounds.

The vestibular system, located in the inner ear, is also crucial for maintaining balance and spatial awareness, helping prevent dizziness and unsteadiness.

Signs of Hearing Loss

Hearing loss can occur gradually over time or happen suddenly, affecting people of all ages. Early detection is crucial for effective treatment. Common signs of hearing loss include:

Difficulty following conversations

Struggling to understand speech, especially in noisy environments or group settings.

Frequently asking others to repeat themselves

Relying on repetition to keep up with conversations.

Turning up the volume 

Increasing the sound on the TV, radio, or phone more than others find comfortable.

Muffled or distorted sounds 

Voices and everyday sounds may seem unclear or distant.

Trouble hearing high-pitched sounds 

Difficulty detecting sounds like birds chirping, doorbells, or alarms.

Tinnitus (ringing or buzzing in the ears) 

A persistent ringing or buzzing noise in one or both ears.

Avoiding social situations 

Withdrawing from conversations and gatherings due to communication difficulties.

Mental exhaustion after listening 

Straining to hear can cause fatigue and cognitive overload.

If you recognize any of these signs, book a hearing check to assess your hearing health and explore possible solutions. Early intervention can improve communication, enhance quality of life, and prevent further hearing deterioration.

Causes of Hearing Loss

Hearing loss can occur due to various factors, including:

  • Aging (Presbycusis) – A natural decline in hearing ability due to aging, often affecting high-frequency sounds first.
  • Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL) – Long-term exposure to loud noises from industrial work, concerts, or personal audio devices can cause permanent inner ear damage.
  • Ear Infections & Blockages – Middle ear infections, fluid buildup, or excessive earwax (cerumen impaction) can temporarily or permanently affect hearing.
  • Genetic & Hereditary Factors – A family history of hearing loss can increase susceptibility to auditory decline.
  • Ototoxic Medications – Certain drugs, including chemotherapy agents, high-dose aspirin, and antibiotics, can cause inner ear damage and hearing impairment.
  • Head Trauma & Neurological Disorders – Injuries to the head or conditions like Ménière’s disease and acoustic neuroma can result in hearing loss, dizziness, and tinnitus.
  • Medical Conditions – Diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and autoimmune disorders can affect blood flow to the inner ear, leading to gradual hearing deterioration.

How Do You Protect Your Hearing?

Protecting your hearing is essential to preventing noise-induced hearing loss, age-related hearing decline, and long-term auditory damage. Here are some key steps to maintain healthy hearing:

  • Use Ear Protection – Wear custom earplugs, noise-canceling headphones, or earmuffs in loud environments such as concerts, construction sites, and industrial workplaces.
  • Lower the Volume – Follow the 60/60 rule, keeping volume levels below 60% and limiting headphone use to 60 minutes per session to reduce the risk of permanent hearing damage.
  • Take Listening Breaks – Give your ears time to recover from loud sounds by stepping away from noisy environments or using quiet zones.
  • Avoid Inserting Objects into Your Ears – Cotton swabs, hairpins, or any other objects can push earwax deeper, leading to impacted earwax, infections, or eardrum damage.
  • Be Aware of Ototoxic Medications – Some antibiotics, chemotherapy drugs, and high-dose painkillers can cause hearing loss and tinnitus. Always consult your doctor about potential side effects.
  • Maintain Overall Health – Keep conditions like diabetes, high blood pressure, and heart disease under control, as they can affect circulation to the inner ear and contribute to hearing decline.
  • Have Regular Hearing Check-ups – Schedule annual hearing tests with a professional audiologist, especially if you are over 50, work in noisy environments, or have a history of hearing loss.

Ear Wax (Cerumen)

Earwax, also known as cerumen, is a natural substance produced by glands in the ear canal. It serves an essential role in maintaining ear health by trapping dust, debris, and bacteria, preventing them from reaching the sensitive inner ear. Earwax also keeps the ear canal lubricated and protected, reducing irritation and infections.

Why Does Earwax Build Up?

While earwax usually moves out of the ear naturally, some factors can cause excessive buildup, including:

  • Overproduction of earwax – Some people naturally produce more wax than others.
  • Frequent headphone or hearing aid use – Devices that block the ear canal can prevent wax from clearing naturally.
  • Using cotton swabs or objects in the ear – These can push wax deeper, causing blockages.
  • Aging – Older adults are more prone to dry, hardened earwax that doesn’t move out easily.

Signs of Earwax Blockage

Excessive earwax buildup can cause various symptoms, including:

  • Temporary hearing loss – Sounds may become muffled or blocked.
  • Ear discomfort or fullness –A clogged sensation in the ear.
  • Tinnitus (ringing in the ears) – A buzzing or ringing sound caused by wax pressing on the eardrum.
  • Dizziness and balance issues – Excess earwax may affect the ear’s ability to regulate balance.
  • Itching or ear pain – Some antibiotics, chemotherapy drugs, and high-dose painkillers can cause hearing loss and tinnitus. Always consult your doctor about potential side effects.
  • Maintain Overall Health – Irritation in the ear canal.

Tinnitus – Ringing in the Ears

Tinnitus is the perception of ringing, buzzing, hissing, or whistling sounds in the ears without an external source. It can occur in one or both ears and may be constant or intermittent. Tinnitus is a common condition, affecting millions of people worldwide, and can range from mild to severe, sometimes interfering with daily activities and sleep.

Common Causes of Tinnitus

Tinnitus can develop due to various factors, including:

Noise-Induced Hearing Loss 

Prolonged exposure to loud sounds from concerts, heavy machinery, or personal audio devices can damage the hair cells in the inner ear, leading to permanent hearing damage and tinnitus.

Age-Related Hearing Loss (Presbycusis) 

Natural deterioration of the auditory system as we age.

Ear Infections & Blockages 

Fluid buildup, excessive earwax (cerumen impaction), or infections can cause temporary tinnitus.

Stress & Anxiety 

High stress levels can exacerbate tinnitus by increasing sensitivity to sound.

Circulatory Problems 

Conditions like high blood pressure, poor circulation, and cardiovascular disease can affect the blood flow to the inner ear, triggering tinnitus.

Ototoxic Medications 

Certain drugs, such as aspirin, antibiotics, chemotherapy agents, and diuretics, can damage the ear and worsen tinnitus symptoms.

Ménière’s Disease 

A disorder affecting the inner ear that can lead to vertigo, tinnitus, and hearing loss.

Jaw & Neck Issues (TMJ Disorders) 

Problems with the temporomandibular joint (TMJ) or cervical spine can contribute to tinnitus.

Symptoms of Tinnitus

People experience tinnitus in different ways, including:

  • High-pitched ringing, buzzing, or hissing sounds
  • Pulsatile tinnitus – A rhythmic sound that matches your heartbeat, often linked to circulation issues
  • Intermittent or constant noise perception
  • Increased sensitivity to sound (hyperacusis)
  • Difficulty concentrating or sleeping due to persistent noise

Managing & Treating Tinnitus

While there is no definitive cure for tinnitus, various strategies can help manage symptoms and improve quality of life:

  • Hearing Aids – For individuals with hearing loss and tinnitus, hearing aids can amplify external sounds, reducing the perception of ringing.
  • Sound Therapy – White noise machines, nature sounds, or low-level background noise can help mask tinnitus and make it less noticeable.
  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) – A psychological approach that helps individuals manage emotional distress caused by tinnitus.
  • Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT) – A combination of sound therapy and counseling to help the brain habituate to tinnitus.
  • Stress Management Techniques – Meditation, breathing exercises, and relaxation techniques can minimize tinnitus-related stress.
  • Lifestyle Adjustments – Reducing caffeine, alcohol, and nicotine intake may improve symptoms.
